## The Philosophy: You Are The Niche

*(Dan Koe)*

Your brand is not a topic. It's a unique synthesis of your interests, your story, and the specific problems you solve. No one else is a UX designer who has shipped design systems across 10 markets, rebuilt their portfolio using Figma Console MCP, and can explain why the question matters more than the answer in an AI-first world.

That combination is the niche. Not "UX designer." Not "AI content creator." The whole person.

---

## The Why

I'm a UI/UX designer who believes most people — including designers — are underestimating how important UX strategy will become, especially as AI changes the industry.

The core idea I keep coming back to:

> **UX is the question. UI is the answer. AI makes the answer easier to get — but asking the right question is harder than ever, and more important than ever.**

That's the bridge between human and AI. The designers who will matter in the future are the ones who know how to ask the right question — not just execute the output.

This shapes everything I write about. I'm not writing from a place of having it all figured out. I'm writing because I think I'm still building my UX strategy depth, and I believe reflecting out loud on how AI affects user flows and experience design is a conversation the industry needs more of.

## The Content Ecosystem

*(Dan Koe)*

Don't build a funnel. Build a world. Content should form an interconnected ecosystem where each tier feeds the others.

### Long-form: Portfolio Blog (Pillars)
The foundation. These are your "Intellectual Signature" — documenting complex AI design workflows in full detail. No one else has this exact thinking. This is where authority builds.

- Depth over frequency
- Every post should be the definitive take on that specific problem
- Live at your portfolio so traffic compounds over time

### Medium-form: LinkedIn Carousels / Articles (Deconstruct)
Take a blog post and break it into a swipeable carousel or short article. Enough value that someone follows you after the first slide. The goal is "instant follow" — competence visible in 30 seconds.

- One carousel per blog post minimum
- Lead with the most surprising or counterintuitive insight from the full piece
- End with a CTA back to the full article

### Short-form: LinkedIn Posts (MVPs)
Daily or near-daily posts that act as Minimum Viable Products for new ideas. Test what resonates before investing in a full article. These are conversational, fast, and often the top of the funnel.

- Use these to float new angles, micro-insights, or questions
- Watch which ones get saved or commented on — those become blog posts
- Don't overthink them. Done beats perfect.

## Core Content Principle: Pain and Process

*(Dan Koe / Bonnie's proven formula)*

Better content is not about raw information. It's about your unique perspective on information. Every piece of content follows this structure:

### The Hook — Pain
Start with a relatable problem. Something the reader has felt, not just heard about. Examples that work:
- The dread of updating a portfolio
- Watching AI generate generic, interchangeable design output
- The fear of being irrelevant instead of irreplaceable
- The gap between knowing a framework and actually using one

### The Transformation — Process
Your specific, step-by-step workflow or thinking that solves the problem. This is where you earn trust. Not theory — what you actually did, with real details.

### The Benefit — Reframe
Clearly state what changes on the other side. Not just "it's faster" but what it means: more time on craft, more control over your work, more confidence in your decisions.

**The proven formula:**
```
Real problem (that many designers feel)
+ Universal relevance (not niche, not gatekept)
+ AI workflow as the concrete solution
= Content worth reading and sharing
```

## Engagement Triggers: The Principles That Stop the Scroll

*(Dan Koe's 10 Commandments, applied to your context)*

Use these deliberately — not all at once, but as a toolkit:

**Specific numbers** — "How I reduced iteration time by 75%" lands harder than "faster." Quantify wherever you can, even if it's approximate.

**Pattern interrupts** — Your actual design work looks different from homogeneous AI output. Show it. Screenshots, before/afters, real screens from real projects.

**Negativity bias** — Address the traps directly. "Why most designers are using AI wrong" or "The portfolio mistake I keep seeing" outperforms positive advice headlines.

**Confidence and conviction** — Speak with authority about where design is going. You've shipped at scale. You've seen what works and what doesn't. Write from that place, not from "maybe" and "I think."

**Curiosity gap** — Start a post with a tension, not a conclusion. Let the reader want to know how it resolves.

## Content Pillars

### 1. UX Strategy & Thinking
The stuff senior designers are expected to use, not just name-drop. Frameworks grounded in real use cases. Always tied to: what does this change about how you design?

### 2. AI x Design (Honest, Practical)
Not "AI will replace you" or "AI is just a tool." The nuanced version: AI shifts the work, it doesn't eliminate it. Practical workflows from real experiments. Honest about what doesn't work too.

### 3. The Design Process, Honestly
What the textbooks say vs. what actually happens. The compressed steps, the trade-offs, the things nobody tells you until you've shipped something at scale.

### 4. Career & Craft
Reflections on growing as a designer — not from a place of expertise, but active learning. The gap between junior and senior isn't years, it's the kind of thinking you do.

## Voice & Tone

- Honest, not performative
- Grounded in specifics — numbers, names of frameworks, real scenarios
- Never preachy or "here's what you should do"
- Curious — writing from the middle of figuring something out, not the end
- Practical — every post leaves the reader with something usable or a new way to think about something
- Conviction — say the thing directly. Hedge less.

## AI as Orchestrator, Not Author

*(Dan Koe)*

Your edge is taste and discernment. AI cannot replicate that.

Use AI to automate the busy work: generating descriptions, researching trends, summarising user interviews, restructuring case studies, formatting blog posts. This frees you for high-level creative direction — the part that actually requires you.

Do not let AI take over the parts of your craft you deeply care about. Content should be **authenticity at scale** — machines amplify your voice, not replace it.

**Longer-term: build a Library of Prompts.** The workflows you've developed (Figma Console MCP → design tokens → rebuild; voice note → structured blog post; Qlik-style case study restructuring) can eventually become templates, mini-courses, or "Learning Experiences" for other designers. That's the productised version of this content strategy.

## Manual Growth: Don't Wait for the Algorithm

*(Dan Koe)*

At 2,000 followers, the platform won't find your audience for you. You have to go get it.

**Join a tribe.** Identify 5-10 larger accounts in design and tech — people exploring AI x design, design systems, or senior-level craft. Leave quality replies consistently. Become a known face in that conversation before you need anything from it.

**Non-needy networking.** Reach out to peers exploring similar AI workflows — not to promote, to share. "I've been experimenting with X, curious what you're finding" opens doors that a pitch never does.

**One strategic post per week.** One high-value deep-dive (AI tutorial, detailed workflow breakdown, case study insight) per week — and ask your network to share it. One ask, one post, every week. That's how you borrow other people's audiences without burning goodwill.

## LinkedIn as Public Town Square, Portfolio Blog as Authority Base

LinkedIn (2,000 followers) = attract attention. Test ideas. Build in public. Short punchy posts and carousels that earn the follow.

Portfolio blog = build authority. Longer pieces that demonstrate intellectual depth. The destination you send people to when they want more.

These two platforms should reference each other constantly. Every blog post becomes LinkedIn content. Every LinkedIn post that performs becomes a blog candidate.
