Jakob Nielsen published these 10 heuristics in 1994. That's over 30 years ago. The web barely existed, smartphones were science fiction, and Figma wouldn't exist for another two decades. And yet, every senior designer you know can recite most of them from memory.

That longevity isn't nostalgia. It's because these principles aren't about technology — they're about how humans perceive, remember, and react to systems. Those things don't change when iOS releases a new version.

This isn't a beginner's intro. If you've been designing for a while, you've applied most of these without naming them. What's useful at this stage is being precise: knowing exactly which heuristic you're violating, and being intentional about when a violation is actually justified.

01 Visibility of System Status

The system should always keep users informed about what is going on, through appropriate feedback within reasonable time.

Put simply: users should never have to wonder if something worked. When they click a button, submit a form, or upload a file, something should visually confirm that the system received the action and is doing something about it.

Where this breaks is usually one of two places: either the feedback is missing entirely (nothing happens after a click), or it's there but too slow — the confirmation arrives 4 seconds later, after the user has already clicked again.

Real world

Instagram's story upload bar, Gmail's "Sending…" → "Sent" state, Figma's multiplayer cursor indicators — all of these exist specifically to satisfy this heuristic. When Linear shows a spinner on a slow network request instead of a frozen UI, that's intentional design, not a nice-to-have.

02 Match Between System and the Real World

The system should speak the users' language. Use words, phrases, and concepts familiar to the user, rather than system-oriented terms. Follow real-world conventions, making information appear in a natural and logical order.

This one is harder than it sounds at mid-level because the failure mode isn't "terrible jargon" — it's subtle mismatch. The difference between a button labelled "Discard" vs "Delete" vs "Remove" vs "Clear" seems minor but creates real cognitive overhead across a session.

Apple named it a "Trash can," not "Pending deletion queue." Zoom added a literal 🤚 "Raise Hand" button because the metaphor transfers perfectly from a physical meeting. Figma calls them "Frames" and "Components" — not "containers" and "templates" — because the former matches how designers already think.

03 User Control and Freedom

Users often choose system functions by mistake and will need a clearly marked "emergency exit" to leave the unwanted state without having to go through an extended dialogue. Support undo and redo.

The deeper principle here is psychological safety. When users know they can undo, they explore more. When every action feels permanent, they slow down, second-guess, and disengage. Good undo is a feature that makes the rest of the product feel better.

Gmail's "Undo Send" is a textbook implementation — you can configure up to 30 seconds. Figma's undo history goes back through an entire session. Notion's Cmd+Z works across block moves, deletions, and even page renames. Notice how all three are high-stakes collaboration tools where accidental actions have real consequences — so the designers invested accordingly.

04 Consistency and Standards

Users should not have to wonder whether different words, situations, or actions mean the same thing. Follow platform conventions.

This operates at two levels: internal consistency (your product is consistent with itself) and external consistency (your product follows conventions users already know). Violating internal consistency creates friction. Violating external consistency creates confusion.

Real world

Cmd+Z for undo works in Figma, Notion, Gmail, VS Code, and your notes app. That's external consistency at its most powerful — you don't think about it, you just do it. Notion's design system is worth studying for internal consistency: every block type behaves the same way when you hover, select, or drag it.

05 Error Prevention

Even better than good error messages is a careful design which prevents a problem from occurring in the first place. Either eliminate error-prone conditions or check for them and present users with a confirmation option before they commit to the action.

This is arguably the most underrated heuristic. Most design teams spend time on error recovery — writing better error messages — when they should be spending it on error prevention. A confirmation modal before a destructive action is worth ten well-worded error messages.

Google Forms won't let you submit without filling in required fields — it highlights them before you even try. Figma prompts "You have unsaved changes" before closing a tab. GitHub asks you to type the repository name before deleting it. The friction is intentional and calibrated to the severity of the action.

06 Recognition Rather Than Recall

Minimize the user's memory load by making objects, actions, and options visible. The user should not have to remember information from one part of an interface to another. Instructions for use of the system should be visible or easily retrievable whenever appropriate.

Human working memory is limited to about 4 items at a time. Every time your interface forces a user to remember something from a previous screen, you're taxing a scarce resource. Recognition (seeing options) is essentially free. Recall (remembering them) is expensive.

Figma's Cmd+/ shows recent files, components, and actions before you type a single character. Notion's / command menu surfaces every block type with icons and descriptions — you don't need to memorise slash commands. Google search shows autocomplete based on what you've searched before. The common thread: surface the most likely next actions proactively.

07 Flexibility and Efficiency of Use

Accelerators — unseen by the novice user — may often speed up the interaction for the expert user such that the system can cater to both inexperienced and experienced users. Allow users to tailor frequent actions.

The key insight: your interface should serve two audiences simultaneously without making either feel like a second-class citizen. Novices need affordances. Experts need speed. Keyboard shortcuts are the classic solution — invisible to beginners, invaluable to power users.

Figma's right-click menu always shows keyboard shortcuts alongside every action — passive education for power users. Notion's toolbar is visible for beginners but / commands are faster for experts. Linear lets you assign issues, change priorities, and set dates entirely from the keyboard. Notice how none of these force you to choose — they accommodate both modes at once.

08 Aesthetic and Minimalist Design

Dialogues should not contain irrelevant or rarely needed information. Every extra unit of information in a dialogue competes with the relevant units of information and diminishes their relative visibility.

This is often misread as "make it look clean." It's actually about information hierarchy and cognitive load. Every element on screen competes for attention. Unnecessary elements don't just fail to help — they actively hurt by diluting signal. The principle isn't aesthetic; it's functional.

Real world

Linear is the clearest modern example. Each issue shows: title, status, assignee, priority. Everything else is one click away. Apple's iOS home screen shows app icons and names — nothing else. Stripe's dashboard surfaces the three numbers you check every day front and centre. Minimalism here isn't visual style — it's a deliberate decision about what information earns real estate.

09 Help Users Recognize, Diagnose, and Recover from Errors

Error messages should be expressed in plain language (no codes), precisely indicate the problem, and constructively suggest a solution.

Three parts: recognise (plain language), diagnose (what went wrong), recover (what to do next). Most error messages only nail the first one. A message that says "Invalid input" tells you something broke — but not what, or how to fix it. That's a single-part error message pretending to be a full one.

Stripe's error messages are genuinely excellent: "Your card was declined. Contact your bank, or try a different card." — that's all three parts. Loom tells you exactly why a recording failed and what to check. Git's error messages are notoriously terrible — opaque codes, no suggested fixes — and it's become an industry joke. The gap between the two is entirely this heuristic.

10 Help and Documentation

Even though it is better if the system can be used without documentation, it may be necessary to provide help and documentation. Any such information should be easy to search, focused on the user's task, list concrete steps to be carried out, and not be too large.

Counterintuitively, this heuristic is often used to justify lazy design ("we'll just document it"). That's a misread. It's the last resort, not the first solution. The right reading: if your interface requires documentation, you've probably already violated one of the previous nine heuristics. But when documentation is genuinely needed, make it contextual, specific, and close to the point of confusion.

Figma shows tooltips with tool names and keyboard shortcuts on every toolbar item when you hover. Notion's empty pages show suggested templates and starter content — guidance without a separate help page. Slack's empty channels show instructions inside the channel itself. All three deliver help exactly at the moment of confusion, not buried in a separate documentation tab.

Using these as a diagnostic tool, not a checklist

The most useful shift in how to apply these is moving from "am I following rule 6?" to "which heuristic am I violating, and is that violation justified?" Nielsen himself acknowledges that sometimes breaking a heuristic is the right call — what matters is that you're breaking it consciously and have a reason.

The common patterns worth memorising: if users make the same mistake repeatedly, you have a #5 problem (error prevention). If users can't find features they know exist, you likely have #6 (recognition) or #8 (minimalist design). If your support tickets are full of "how do I..." questions, check #10 first, then #3 and #6.

These 10 principles took Jakob Nielsen years of research to distil. Thirty years later, the teams building the best products in the world still design with them in mind. That's not tradition — it's just that the problems haven't changed.
