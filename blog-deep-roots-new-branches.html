The designers who win long-term have timeless foundations and a curious eye for what's changing. Here's how to build both.

There's a question I keep coming back to in conversations with other designers: how much do you invest in learning something new versus deepening what you already know?

The framing is usually wrong. It's not either/or. It's a two-layer system — and the healthiest designers I know have both.

The Two-Layer System

Think of your design practice like a tree. The roots go deep — slow to grow, invisible to most, but the reason you don't fall over when the wind changes. The branches reach outward — always moving, responsive to light, visibly alive.

One without the other doesn't work. Roots without branches is a stump. Branches without roots is just... kindling.

"The designers who win long-term are the ones with deep roots and curious branches."

Layer 1: First Principles

Layer 1 — The Bottom Layer
Things that won't change in 20 years

Human psychology. Decision-making. Trust. Hierarchy. Contrast. Empathy as a research skill. The ability to frame a problem before solving it.

First principles are grounded in human nature, which is ancient. Why people make decisions doesn't change. What makes something feel trustworthy, clear, or delightful has been the same for centuries. The way the eye moves across a composition. The brain's need to group and categorize. The discomfort of ambiguity. The satisfaction of completion.

The further you go in your career, the more you realize how much of "good design" is just applied psychology dressed up in different tools. This is why a designer with ten years of experience can look at a new interface they've never seen before and immediately know what's wrong with it. They're not pattern-matching against a trend. They're reading human behavior.

Hierarchy. Contrast. Whitespace. Framing the right problem before opening Figma. Empathy as something you practice in every user conversation, not just when it's in the brief. The ability to walk into a room, understand what everyone actually needs, and translate it into something concrete and shippable.

None of this has an expiry date.

Layer 2: Current Signal

Layer 2 — The Top Layer
Things that are changing fast — right now

Tools, workflows, formats, platforms, interaction patterns. The things that make you competent and relevant in this particular moment.

Five years ago the current signal was Figma replacing Sketch. Two years ago it was AI going from "interesting research" to "in every job description." Today it's prompting, building, agentic workflows, and understanding exactly where the human still needs to drive.

These change constantly. Your job isn't to master every new thing — it's to quickly evaluate whether something is worth your time, or just noise.

The signal worth chasing is the kind that changes how you work, not just what you click. AI isn't just a new tool — it changed the process itself. That's signal. A new plugin for color palettes? That's noise. The difference is whether it reshapes your thinking or just adds a shortcut.

The Traps

There's a trap on each side, and I've seen talented designers fall into both.

Only following trends

You're always chasing. Exhausted, reactive, and shallow. You mistake fluency with tools for actual design thinking. And when the tools change — and they will — you panic. You've built your identity around being "good at X" where X is a product or a workflow, not a skill. It feels like being current, but it's actually fragile.

Only studying first principles

You become irrelevant. Great taste, nothing to apply it to. You sound wise in interviews and then struggle to ship anything at the pace the job requires. You judge the designers chasing trends without realizing you've stopped doing the work that earns you the right to have opinions. Theory without velocity is just philosophy.

"If you only chase trends, you're always chasing. If you only study principles, you become irrelevant."

How to Build Both

For the roots: read outside design. Psychology, behavioral economics, systems thinking, storytelling. The best designers I know have read Thinking, Fast and Slow more recently than the latest design systems article. Study interfaces that have stood the test of time — not for inspiration, but to understand why they worked. Ask the mechanism, not just admire the outcome.

For the branches: stay in the practice. Build things, not just moodboards. Use AI tools until you have an actual opinion on what they're good at and where they fall down. Follow the people who are one step ahead in your specific area — not to copy their aesthetic, but to understand what problems they're working on.

And when you're unsure whether something new is worth your time? Ask yourself: does this change how I think, or just how I click?

The first kind is always worth learning. It deepens the roots.

The second kind — stay curious, but don't make it your identity.
