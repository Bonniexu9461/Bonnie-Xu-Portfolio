How I Increased Domino's Weekly Sales by $57K Through Design

Making it easy for customers was worth an extra $57K a week. Here's exactly what we changed, and why it worked.

One of the biggest lessons I learned while leading web UI at Domino's wasn't actually about design. It was about human psychology. Every extra step you put in front of a customer has a literal price tag, and we found out exactly how much ours was costing.

The Problem: A Page That Was Working Against Us

Domino's had rebranded, but only the homepage reflected the new identity. The Offers page, one of the highest-traffic pages on the site, still ran on legacy IA. When I dug into the analytics, the numbers were hard to ignore.

67% of users were abandoning the Offers page before they ever saw a single deal. Not because the deals were bad. Not because the design was ugly. They were leaving because of a location gate — a data entry step that required your address before showing you anything of value. We were asking people to give before we gave.

Heatmap analysis showed a second problem: CTAs weren't clearly prioritised, so users who did push through the gate weren't converting at the rate we expected. And in user testing, 5 out of 8 participants misclassified delivery vs pickup offers, differentiated by colour alone with no labels.

Three Changes That Moved the Needle

1. Show Value First

We removed the location gate from the top of the page entirely. National offers (the deals that apply everywhere, no address required) were surfaced immediately when users landed. Local, store-specific offers sat below, gated behind location input only where genuinely necessary.

The logic was simple: lead with what you can offer right now. Don't make someone prove they're a worthy customer before you show them the menu.

2. Clean the Clutter

We restructured the information architecture so users didn't need to navigate away or click through redirects to browse deals. Everything surfaced in a clean, scannable in-page layout. Fewer decisions, fewer steps, faster path to "add to cart."

3. Clarity Over Colour

Delivery vs pickup offers were redesigned with clear, explicit labels, not just different background colours. This seems minor, but for new users unfamiliar with the brand conventions, it eliminated a significant source of confusion. When people understand what they're clicking, they click.

We validated through A/B testing in the Netherlands before rolling out to Australia and beyond. The results confirmed what the research suggested.

The Principle Behind It

I call it "Value First" design. Your website, or any digital product, should never be a barrier. It shouldn't ask users to work before they understand why it's worth their time. It should lead with what it offers, and earn the right to ask for information in return.

This principle scales beyond e-commerce. Every form that demands details before showing a preview, every paywall that blocks before explaining value, every onboarding flow that gates features before users have felt the product. These are all location gates in disguise.

The most important question you can ask when reviewing any user journey is: What is this screen asking the user to give, and what have we given them first?
