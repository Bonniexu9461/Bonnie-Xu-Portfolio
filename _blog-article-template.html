The Hidden Cost of Ignoring Accessibility in Product Design

Why building for the 15% who are disabled makes your product better for the other 85% too — and how to start today.

Over the past decade working in product and UX design, I've sat in dozens of sprint planning sessions where accessibility was treated as a nice-to-have — something to be "tackled in a future sprint." In reality, that future sprint rarely comes. And the cost? Far higher than most teams realise.

Globally, about 1 in 6 people live with some form of disability. That's over 1 billion people. If your product isn't accessible, you're not just failing a moral test — you're leaving a huge market on the table and creating technical debt that compounds over time.

The Curb-Cut Effect

In the 1970s, disability activists lobbied for curb cuts — the small ramps that transition a footpath to the road level. Initially seen as a "special accommodation," they turned out to benefit everyone: parents with prams, delivery workers with carts, cyclists, elderly people, and travellers with rolling luggage.

The same principle applies to digital accessibility. Closed captions were designed for the Deaf community. Today, they're used by millions watching videos in noisy cafés, learning a second language, or simply preferring to read along. Keyboard navigation, originally for motor-impaired users, is heavily used by power users and developers who never touch a mouse.

Where Most Teams Go Wrong

The most common mistake I see is treating accessibility as a visual checklist: colour contrast ratios, alt text on images, aria labels on icons. These are necessary — but they're only the surface layer.

The four POUR principles

The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) organise accessibility around four principles, commonly known as POUR:

  • Perceivable — Information must be presentable in ways users can perceive (e.g. text alternatives for images, sufficient colour contrast).
  • Operable — Interface components must be operable (e.g. full keyboard navigation, enough time to complete tasks).
  • Understandable — Information and UI operation must be understandable (e.g. consistent navigation, clear error messages).
  • Robust — Content must be robust enough to be interpreted by a wide variety of user agents, including assistive technologies.

Most teams nail the first principle. Operable and understandable are where things typically fall apart — especially in complex, multi-step flows like checkout, onboarding, or settings management.

Practical Starting Points

Here's how I recommend starting without overhauling your entire design system at once:

  1. Run an automated audit using a tool like axe DevTools or Lighthouse. This catches roughly 30–40% of issues and gives you a baseline.
  2. Conduct a keyboard-only navigation test. Try completing your core user flow with zero mouse input. Where does it break?
  3. Test with a screen reader (VoiceOver on Mac, NVDA on Windows). Even 20 minutes will reveal issues you'd never catch visually.
  4. Audit your colour palette for WCAG AA contrast ratios — 4.5:1 for body text, 3:1 for large text and UI components.

Building It Into Your Process

The real shift happens when accessibility moves from a QA checklist to a design value. This means:

  • Including accessibility criteria in your Definition of Done
  • Inviting people with disabilities to user research and usability testing sessions
  • Adding semantic HTML tokens to your design system documentation
  • Advocating for accessibility budget in roadmap conversations — it's significantly cheaper to build right than retrofit later

In my experience, the teams that do this consistently don't just produce more accessible products — they produce better products overall. The rigour required for accessibility forces clarity in information architecture, interaction design, and content strategy.
